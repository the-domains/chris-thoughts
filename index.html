Slicing Pie

By Mike Moyer

Cause #1 of start-up failure is bad partnership. The author proposes a practical yet structured manner to account for the various contributions of the founders in early stage start-ups. Also, you'll realise how important it is to share the benefits with those sharing the risks with you.

The Art of the Long View

By Peter Schwartz

Great book about scenario planning. It will help you develop a strategic vision within your business. The approach has been adopted by Shell a long time ago.

It makes an interesting pair with "The Fifth Discipline".

Hunting capital way too early

#3 mistake leading to start-up failure

According to stats compiled by Josh Lerner from Thomson/Reuter data on US venture capitalists, 30% VCs have a negative return whereas approximately 10% deliver a decent return (25% p.a). Yeah, some get more than 500% return per annum. But imagine what happens to this return if they had not the Google, Twitter, Facebook, etc. in their portfolio?

Don't take persistence for resilience

#2 mistake leading to start-up failure

Business plan, business model… are tools to help you think about everything you need to. They are no end per se, just a mean. The only judge of your product/service is the customer.

Choosing the right business partners

#1 mistake leading to start-up failure

Brother, sister, dad, mom: avoid. You don't want to yell at each other for business. They won't ever be completely honest with you, especially if they think your idea sucks. Just as you'll probably be very supportive of their initiative. In-laws too: avoid.

4 traps entrepreneurs can avoid

9 startups out of 10 fails. According to stats in the US, 50% companies don't last more than 5 years. In Europe, from Eurostar data, one gets a failure rate of 25-33% within the 2 first years. Why such a discrepancy? Well, not all startups get incorporated. Many aborts even before reaching that point.
